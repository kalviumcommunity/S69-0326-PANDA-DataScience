01

Revenue by Category

Which product categories generate the most value across the retail chain?

📊 Chart Explanation

Key Insight: Home & Kitchen leads in total revenue despite having the same number of SKUs as other categories. This is driven by high-price items like Air Fryers and Coffee Makers. Electronics follows closely. Groceries generate the least revenue due to low unit prices — but they sell in higher volumes. Recommendation: Prioritize stock availability for Home & Kitchen items to protect the highest-revenue category.
02

Sales Trend Over Time

How does overall demand evolve across the year? Are there seasonal peaks?

📊 Chart Explanation

Key Insight: Sales volume peaks significantly in Q4 (October–December), likely driven by holiday shopping. There is a secondary dip in mid-year months (June–August). Recommendation: Begin increasing safety stock levels from September to prepare for the Q4 surge. Promotions can be used to stimulate demand during slow mid-year months.
03

Stockout Events by Store

Which stores are most vulnerable to inventory shortfalls?

📊 Chart Explanation

Key Insight: Store A records the highest number of stockout events. This suggests either inadequate replenishment schedules or consistently underestimated demand. Action: Review supplier lead times and reorder points for Store A specifically.

Product Movement Classification

Which products are flying off the shelves — and which are collecting dust?

📊 Chart Explanation

Key Insight: Products are classified into slow, medium, and fast movers using the 33rd and 66th percentile thresholds. Products below the red dashed line are slow-movers — they represent tied-up capital. Products above the green line are candidates for increased stock levels to prevent stockouts.

05

Demand Variability

How predictable is demand across product categories?

📊 Chart Explanation

Key Insight: The boxplot shows the spread of weekly units sold per category. Wider boxes and more outliers indicate high variability — making demand harder to forecast. Groceries and Sports show the widest spreads, meaning retailers must hold higher safety stock for these categories. Electronics shows more consistent demand with fewer outliers.

📊 Chart Explanation — Sales Heatmap: Store × Month

Key Insight: The heatmap reveals spatial-temporal patterns in sales. Darker cells = higher volume. This helps identify which store-month combinations are underperforming. Stores consistently light-colored in certain months may benefit from targeted promotions or temporary stock reallocation from other stores.
06

Key Findings & Recommendations

Actionable insights derived from the full year of data analysis.

📉

Stockouts Cost Revenue

With a 13% stockout rate, the chain is losing potential sales weekly. Fast-moving SKUs need tighter reorder triggers.

🚀

Q4 Is the Money Season

Sales spike in Q4. Inventory planning must begin in September — not November — to avoid running out of stock during peak demand.

📦

Dead Stock Drains Capital

Slow-moving products in the bottom tercile represent cash tied up in unsold goods. Promotions or reduced orders can free this capital.

🏪

Store A Needs Attention

Store A has the most stockout events — likely a logistics or reorder-point issue, not a demand issue. Local audit recommended.

Methodology

How this analysis was built — from raw data to insights.

Problem Setup

Defined research questions, loaded raw CSV, explored shape and schema with Pandas.

W2

Data Cleaning

Removed duplicates, fixed inconsistent casing, handled missing values with median imputation.

EDA & Visualization

Insights

Assumptions & Limitations: Dataset is synthetic and generated for educational purposes. Demand types were pre-assigned during simulation — real-world data would require unsupervised clustering (e.g., K-Means) to classify products. No external variables such as promotions, competitor pricing, or economic indicators were modeled. Results should be validated against actual operational data before making business decisions.
